# Pangenomics: From a Single Reference to a Multi-Genome Resource

## Overview
A conceptual review of pangenomics as I see it.

---

## 1. Introduction: representation and use

A pangenome is often presented as a response to a technical problem. The field had relied on a single reference genome, that reference was distorting biological inference, and a collection of genomes promised to reduce the distortion. This is true, but incomplete, because it casts the pangenome as an improved reference rather than the fundamentally different resource it is.

This review is organized around one claim: a pangenome serves two distinct purposes, and nearly every concept in the field follows from one or the other.

The first is representational. A pangenome is a record of evolutionary history. Its sequences are related by descent, so the structure recovered by aligning them is not arbitrary; it carries the history that produced it, and in practice the ancestral state of almost any locus is present somewhere within it. In this sense a pangenome is a means of studying variation itself, of describing how a given stretch of sequence relates to its own copies across a population.

The second purpose is operational, and it motivates much of the field's current work. A pangenome is not only read but used. It is a structured store of genomic segments that can be recombined, compressed, indexed, and queried, and used in this way it becomes an instrument for genomics on individuals: reconstructing a genome, quantifying expression, calling structural variants, recovering function that no single reference can represent. In this mode the pangenome is an object to be manipulated rather than a reference to be consulted.

These two purposes are not in conflict. They are the same object approached for different ends, and the field's two dominant construction strategies are distinguished largely by which of the two they prioritize. Both are maintained throughout the discussion that follows.

## 2. From one reference to many

To see why pangenomes arose, one must first understand what they replace, and that begins not with a genome but with a workflow.

**The resequencing paradigm.** Modern human genomics is, overwhelmingly, resequencing. We rarely read a person's chromosomes from end to end. Instead, intact genomic DNA is sheared into fragments, those fragments are prepared into sequenceable libraries, the libraries are read chemically into short reads, and the reads are interpreted by placing them against a genome already in hand. That genome is the reference. Reads are mapped to it, and from the resulting pile-up an individual's genomic landscape is reconstructed: where they match the reference, where they differ, and how. This reference-guided reconstruction is, in practice, what genomics is in the clinic and in most of research. The case for pangenomes is in part a case about what this step gets wrong.

**How alignment works.** Underpinning read mapping lies the most basic operation in computational biology, sequence alignment, and a single worked example is enough to convey it. The local case is the right one to teach, because it is simpler to motivate and because it is the operation read mapping actually performs. The Smith-Waterman algorithm finds the best-matching subsequence between two sequences rather than forcing them into end-to-end correspondence, which is exactly the question of where in a large reference a short read belongs. Its global counterpart, Needleman-Wunsch, aligns sequences in their entirety; that colinear, whole-sequence form returns later, when whole assemblies are aligned to one another (Section 5). One example of local alignment is therefore sufficient, and the aim at this stage is modest: a first, honest glimpse of how comparative-biological reasoning becomes computation.

**The human reference and the framing of diversity.** The human reference genome is not a person. It is a mosaic drawn from a small number of donors and dominated by one, progressively patched and upgraded across two decades. Whatever its merits, it established a single linear coordinate system and a single set of alleles as the background against which all human variation would be described. Diversity, in effect, became defined relative to one idiosyncratic sequence.

**The variant-catalog era.** The field's first answer to this was not a pangenome but a catalog. The 1000 Genomes Project, dbSNP, and related efforts recorded human variation as lists of differences, typically stored in VCF, layered over the reference coordinates. This is an important way station, because a reference together with a comprehensive catalog of population variants is already a flattened, implicit pangenome. It concedes that one sequence is insufficient; it simply keeps the variation in a side file rather than in the genome model itself. It is also where reference bias was first felt acutely, since a variant can be cataloged only if it can be expressed against the reference to begin with.

**Why a single reference is insufficient.** That observation generalizes into the central motivation for pangenomics. A linear reference can describe only what it can represent. Sequence absent from the reference, whether an insertion common in the population but missing from the donor, a structural rearrangement, an expanded repeat array, or an entire gene, has no coordinates, and so for practical purposes does not exist as far as the analysis is concerned. Reads from such sequence map poorly, fail to map, or map confidently to the wrong location. This is reference bias: a systematic distortion in which measurement is drawn toward the single genome that happened to be chosen, and in which the most variable, most interesting, and often most medically important regions are seen least well. A pangenome is the attempt to remove the privileged status of that one sequence.

## 3. What a pangenome is

**A first definition.** A pangenome represents the genomic content of many individuals of a species at once, constructed so that sequence shared among them and sequence that varies among them are both first-class elements of the model. Where a reference answers what the genome looks like, a pangenome answers what a species' genome looks like in its variety.

**The microbial origin.** The term comes from microbiology (Tettelin and colleagues, 2005), and the original concept remains the cleanest point of entry. Comparing multiple isolates of a bacterium, that work distinguished the core genome, present in every isolate, from the dispensable or accessory genome, present in some and absent in others, with the union of all of it forming the pangenome. This raises a quantitative question: as more isolates are sequenced, does the catalog of new genes keep growing, or does it saturate? A pangenome that continues to grow is open; one that saturates is closed, and the shape of that accumulation curve is itself a statement about a species' genomic flexibility. The gene presence and absence framing, and the open and closed distinction, predate any notion of a graph and still underlie how the field reasons.

**At heart, a multiple sequence alignment.** Beneath the data structures, a pangenome is a multiple sequence alignment: a statement of which positions across many genomes correspond. Everything else is a decision about how to store, structure, and query that correspondence. This is worth holding onto, because the vocabulary of graphs can make pangenomes sound categorically new when they are, at bottom, a familiar object, an alignment, at unfamiliar scale.

**Graphs as one realization, and the implicit graph.** The most visible way to structure that alignment is as a pangenome graph, in which sequence shared across genomes is traversed as a common path and variation appears as branches. A graph is one realization of the alignment, however, not a synonym for the pangenome. A pangenome need not be resolved into an explicit, genome-wide graph to be a pangenome. This is the idea of the implicit graph: the pangenome is kept as a queryable alignment, and the graph is resolved only locally, on demand, for the region of interest, without ever constructing the whole structure. It is a conceptually economical move, and it recurs later as a practical necessity (Section 6).

**Haplotypes.** A pangenome compares haplotypes, the sequences inherited together along a single chromosome copy, rather than genotypes blurred across the two copies an individual carries. This matters even when the organism is not human, and it matters most for humans, where each person is a pair of haplotypes and where assigning variation to the correct copy is essential. A pangenome is, stated cleanly, a structured comparison across many haplotypes drawn from a species.

**Evolutionary history, encoded indirectly.** Because those haplotypes are related by descent, the pangenome is not merely a list of observed sequences but an encoding of the evolutionary history that produced them. The structure is observed in the present, yet present-day diversity is the product of past mutation, recombination, and drift, and that history is therefore legible in the structure. In practice, the ancestral state of a given locus can almost always be found among the haplotypes in the graph.

**The recombinable collection.** The most consequential point follows from combining the preceding ones. A pangenome is not only an archive of the individuals used to build it. Because it is a structured collection of genomic segments related by descent, those segments can be re-strung along new paths to represent individuals who were never part of the build. A pangenome therefore does two things at once: it represents a population, and it provides a better representation of any single individual, including one it has never seen. That second capability is the basis of genome inference and personalization (Section 9), and it is what raises a pangenome from a richer reference to a working resource.

## 4. Genomic anatomy and its effect on construction

Before discussing how pangenomes are built, a working picture of what genomes are made of is necessary, because the construction algorithms succeed or fail according to the local anatomy of the sequence they are given.

**Two modes of reconstruction.** First, a distinction that is reliably conflated. Reference-guided resequencing (Section 2) reconstructs an individual by mapping reads to an existing genome. *De novo* assembly builds a genome without a reference, by overlapping and joining the reads themselves. Pangenomes sit at the seam between the two: they are built from *De novo* assemblies of many individuals, and once built they become the target to which future reads are mapped. Naming both modes, and their contrast, early prevents considerable confusion later.

**Chromosomal anatomy.** A chromosome is not uniform sequence. It carries telomeres at its ends and a centromere near its middle, satellite arrays and microsatellites, tandem duplications and gene copy-number arrays, and regions of elevated recombination that confer local instability. These features are functional, but the point that matters here is that each evolves under different rules. Repetitive and self-similar regions mutate, expand, contract, and undergo gene conversion in ways that ordinary unique sequence does not, and they therefore differ in how much they vary between individuals and how similar they are to themselves.

**Transposable elements.** Transposable elements warrant separate mention, both because they constitute a large fraction of many genomes and because they are a major source of insertion variation and self-similarity. They also carry their own functional machinery, including promoters and CpG islands, a fact that becomes important when considering what is lost by ignoring non-reference sequence (Section 11).

**Synteny.** Finally, the concept that makes whole-genome comparison possible: synteny, the conservation of sequence order and orientation along chromosomes between individuals or species. Aligning assemblies to one another is largely the act of establishing syntenic correspondence, and the breakdown of synteny is precisely where construction becomes difficult.

**Why this governs the algorithms.** The conclusion of this section is that genomic anatomy dictates algorithm behavior. Self-similarity, repeat content, copy number, and divergence are not incidental; they are the variables that determine whether a method collapses related sequence cleanly, duplicates it spuriously, or finds the syntenic anchors it requires. Sections 5 and 6 examine how the two dominant methods respond to exactly these features.

## 5. Building pangenomes: two paradigms

Given a set of de novo assemblies, how is a pangenome built from them? Current practice offers three named tools but really two paradigms.

**The Minigraph lineage.** Minigraph builds a graph by progressively adding assemblies to a growing structure, concentrating on larger structural variation and producing a relatively coarse but tractable graph. Minigraph-Cactus extends this lineage by applying the Cactus progressive aligner on top, refining the graph to base-level resolution so that small variants as well as large ones are represented. Because Minigraph-Cactus is the workhorse for base-resolution human pangenomes, much of the discussion of properties in Section 6 concerns it specifically.

**The PGGB paradigm.** The PanGenome Graph Builder takes a different approach. Rather than progressively adding genomes to a reference-anchored structure, it computes all-versus-all alignments among the input sequences and induces a graph from them, treating all genomes symmetrically and modeling explicitly how sequence relates to itself. The result is a graph with markedly different properties, discussed next.

**Assembly-to-assembly alignment.** Both paradigms rest on aligning whole assemblies to one another, the colinear, synteny-based form of alignment anticipated in Section 2. This is where tools such as minimap2 and the concept of synteny do their work, and where the breakdown of syntenic correspondence in divergent or repetitive regions first becomes consequential.

## 6. Properties and trade-offs of the two paradigms

This is the core of the technical material, because the two paradigms make opposite choices, and those choices propagate into everything that can subsequently be done with the graph.

**Minigraph-Cactus.** Minigraph-Cactus has several defining properties. It handles divergence up to a point and then clips: where haplotypes are too diverged for the aligner to establish confident synteny, the graph cannot collapse the separate assemblies together, and those unresolved regions, carried as large stretches holding each assembly's sequence in parallel, are clipped out as unusable for many downstream purposes. There is, in other words, a real ceiling on how much divergence a pangenome can faithfully represent, and because the progressive aligner was not designed for cross-species construction, Minigraph-Cactus pangenomes are effectively within-species objects. The method also encodes variation in a deliberately limited vocabulary of substitutions and insertions or deletions. A copy-number expansion is not recognized as the reuse of a segment; instead the graph grows by laying down additional segments, because the method is largely unaware of self-sequence similarity. An inversion or translocation is not encoded as such, but surfaces as inserted sequence. The compensating advantage is decisive: a Minigraph-Cactus graph is very nearly a directed acyclic graph, and acyclicity permits a topological sort, which permits indexing, which permits aligning reads against the graph. Minigraph-Cactus trades faithful representation of sequence structure for computational usability.

**PGGB.** PGGB makes the opposite trade. Because it models self-similarity explicitly, it collapses repeated sequence back onto itself rather than duplicating it, so a copy-number array becomes a path that traverses a segment several times. This yields a far more faithful picture of how a genome relates to itself and to others, but it makes the graph highly cyclic. Cyclicity defeats topological sorting, which defeats the indexing on which read alignment depends, so PGGB graphs generally cannot serve as targets for alignment. PGGB also scales poorly: as the number of input assemblies grows, the all-versus-all computation and the resulting structure become unwieldy, which is the practical pressure behind the implicit-graph strategy introduced in Section 3, in which the pangenome is kept as a queryable alignment and graphs are resolved locally rather than maintained genome-wide.

**The trade-off, stated plainly.** Minigraph-Cactus is more usable and less faithful to sequence structure; PGGB is more faithful and less usable. One is the appropriate tool for operating on genomic data through alignment; the other is the appropriate tool for understanding the sequence itself. The division maps directly onto the two purposes of Section 1: PGGB serves the representational purpose, Minigraph-Cactus the operational one.

**Formats.** Working with either kind of graph requires fluency in its file formats: the GFA family, including reference-anchored rGFA, the conventions for paths and walks, and the alignment and index formats that accompany them. A graduate treatment should introduce these explicitly here, since they are the medium in which all subsequent manipulation occurs.

**The loss of a single coordinate system.** One conceptual consequence deserves separate statement, because it is among the first genuine difficulties students encounter. A linear reference gives every base a single, universal address. A graph removes that guarantee. The question of where a variant is located no longer has one answer; position becomes a matter of node identities and offsets, of which path is being traversed, and of stable-coordinate conventions layered back on top. This is not a nuisance to be set aside; it is the reason linearization and surjection (Section 11) exist, and it is better presented as their motivation than as an afterthought.

## 7. Visualization and exploration

Before a pangenome can be reasoned about, it must be seen, and visualization is also where students first gain hands-on fluency. Two toolkits dominate practical work with graphs: vg, the variation-graph toolkit, and ODGI. Between them they provide the operations for indexing, querying, transforming, and extracting from pangenome graphs.

The visualization problem is one of scale. A whole-genome graph is far too large to render, so work proceeds locally. Using vg chunk to extract a region small enough to handle on a laptop, the resulting subgraph can be drawn with Bandage, which renders nodes and branching structure in a form that makes bubbles, repeats, and divergence legible. This extract-then-visualize loop is a natural first exercise in graph literacy.

Graph views are complemented by pairwise views. For a region of interest, generating a genome-to-genome alignment with minimap2 and then visualizing the structural relationship between two haplotypes, for example with SVbyEye, exposes inversions, duplications, and rearrangements between a specific pair in a form that is often more interpretable than the graph itself. These pairwise comparisons can be carried into a genome browser: by converting the alignment output of the construction process, such as a HAL file, into a genome-alignment format such as MAF, one can build alignment tracks for pairs of individuals and load them into the WashU Epigenome Browser, viewing the genomes that compose a small pangenome alongside functional data.

One caveat belongs here, and it is conceptual rather than merely technical: these alignments are not unique. The genome alignment implied by a Minigraph-Cactus build depends on the order in which assemblies were added, making it a path-dependent product, and it will differ from an alignment computed directly with minimap2. There is no single true alignment to recover, and students should understand that any such picture is one of several defensible reconstructions.

## 8. The pangenome as manipulable sequence

From here the review separates the two operational uses that most fully express the second purpose from Section 1. They may be taught in either order; this section takes the representational route first.

**Gene graphs.** A natural and especially visualizable construction is the gene graph, or pan-gene: a pangenome centered not on whole chromosomes but on the copies of a gene and its paralogs within a species. Because it is small and bounded, it is easy to draw and to reason about, and it makes copy-number relationships directly visible. It carries one caveat worth stating openly: it takes a gene-centric view of biology, which from the standpoint of non-coding regulation is only part of the story. As a bridge concept, however, it is excellent.

**Pan-transcriptomes.** That bridge leads to the pan-transcriptome: projecting the splicing structure of a transcriptome onto a pangenome graph to build a representation against which expression can be quantified. Here the pangenome ceases to be a picture of variation and becomes an apparatus for measurement.

**Manipulation as the point.** The principle this section is meant to convey is that a pangenome graph is a means of manipulating genomic sequence, not only of depicting it. The relationships among sequences are real and worth studying, but the deeper utility of the graph is that it allows genomic content to be recombined, compressed, and partitioned in order to accomplish concrete tasks, among them the quantification of expression and the resolution of allele-specific function. Construction is one way of working that material.

## 9. Alignment and genome inference

The other operational route begins from a hard problem and arrives at one of the field's more striking ideas.

**Why aligning to a graph is hard.** It is tempting to assume that a graph containing all known sequence is the ideal alignment target. It is not. Exposing an aligner to every possible allele degrades accuracy: irrelevant sequence, meaning alleles the sample does not carry, gives reads spurious places to land and actively harms placement. The opposite error is equally damaging, since relevant sequence the sample does carry cannot be placed if it is absent from the target. Alignment, properly understood, is the means by which an individual's genomic landscape is reconstructed, and that reconstruction is harmed both by missing the right content and by including the wrong content. The objective is therefore not maximal content but appropriate content: the closest available approximation of the individual's genome.

**Pangenome personalization.** This is achieved through a form of genome inference called pangenome personalization. Recall the central idea from Section 3: a pangenome is a recombinable collection of segments from which non-sampled individuals can be reconstructed. Personalization realizes this concretely. By comparing the k-mers present in the graph with the k-mers present in an individual's reads, one selects the pair of traversals, the two recombinant haplotypes, through the pangenome that best represents that diploid individual. Those personalized paths constitute a stitched-together draft assembly of the person, assembled not from their reads alone but from the pangenome's library of known sequence, guided by their reads.

**Two results.** Personalization yields two things at once. First, it produces a compact, sample-appropriate target that makes subsequent alignment substantially more accurate, which is the practical prerequisite for using pangenomes in real pipelines. Second, it is already a first-pass approximation of the individual's genome, obtained at little additional cost. That this works at all is a substantive statement about the population that built the graph. It is well illustrated outside the human case: our work with the mouse pangenome shows how inference reconstructs an unsampled individual from the recombined segments of others, and using a non-human example keeps attention on the mechanism rather than on assumptions about human relatedness.

**Tools.** Concretely, k-mer-based methods carry this out. PanGenie infers genotypes, including for structural and complex variation, directly from k-mers without conventional alignment, and Locityper applies a related k-mer strategy to the targeted genotyping of complex, medically relevant loci. Both are instances of the same inferential idea.

## 10. Variation, genotyping, and downstream analysis

The uses of a pangenome that have accumulated across the review can now be consolidated. There are three. The first is the study of variation itself: using a graph, and the PGGB approach in particular, to understand how genomic sequences relate to themselves and to one another. The second is alignment: using the pangenome as a target against which to place an individual's reads in order to study that individual's sequence and function. The third is genome inference: using the pangenome to reconstruct an approximation of an individual's genome, as in personalization.

These connect to concrete genotyping operations. Once reads are aligned to a graph, vg call can genotype an individual against it, which makes structural variants detectable, including from short-read data, where structural variation has traditionally been hardest to recover. After alignment, a choice arises. One option is to continue working on the graph, in graph coordinates, which preserves the most information. The other is to surject the alignments to linear coordinates so that the large existing ecosystem of linear methods can be applied, at a real cost in information. Linearized in this way, conventional variant calling becomes possible, in which case the pangenome's contribution reduces to improving the accuracy of read placement. The same machinery also supports building a high-quality germline estimate for an individual, against which somatic variants can subsequently be called. In our view the long-term future of the field lies on the graph rather than in routine linearization, but both are presently in use and both should be taught.

## 11. Functional analysis on the graph and surjection

The richest use of a pangenome is to carry functional measurement into sequence that a reference cannot represent, and this is where the representational and operational purposes meet.

**On-graph functional analysis.** In principle, gene expression, DNA methylation, and peak-based assays such as ATAC-seq can all be analyzed directly on the graph, in graph space. In practice the methods for doing so remain immature: the field has graph-native data structures well ahead of graph-native analyses, and much functional processing therefore still requires linear coordinates at some stage.

**Haplotype-specific surjection.** The bridge between the two is haplotype-specific surjection, a term we introduce for this operation, which takes annotations or signals defined on the graph and linearizes them onto the specific pair of haplotypes selected for an individual, or otherwise converts graph-space annotations into linear-space ones. A tool implementing this operation is available. Surjection in this form makes on-graph measurement compatible with the linear analytical world without discarding the advantages of the graph.

**Why measure on the graph at all.** The motivation for measuring on the graph is the payoff that justifies the effort. A reference backbone is blind to sequence it does not contain. Inserted sequences, and transposable elements in particular, carry their own functional features, including promoters and CpG islands and other regulatory elements, and if measurement is confined to the reference, none of what occurs within them can be observed. The inability to measure function across non-reference sequence is among the most consequential limitations of reference-based genomics, and recovering that function, over sequence the reference cannot express, is precisely what a pangenome makes possible. Here the two purposes of Section 1 become one: the structure that represents evolutionary history is the same structure that permits measurement of function where it actually occurs.

## 12. Population and clinical applications

Two further dimensions belong in a complete graduate treatment, though they would not appear in an introductory workshop.

**Population genetics.** Once a pangenome holds many haplotypes, it carries allele frequencies, not merely alleles. This turns it into a resource for population genetics and for association studies: variation that was effectively invisible to genotyping arrays and to linear references, including structural variants, repeat expansions, and the presence or absence of whole segments, becomes representable and therefore testable against phenotype. The pangenome thus extends the reach of association studies into the classes of variation that the reference-and-VCF era handled worst.

**Complex medically relevant loci.** The clinical value is sharpest at a specific and teachable set of loci: the HLA and MHC region, the KIR cluster, the immunoglobulin loci, large segmental duplications, and repeat-expansion disease genes. These share two properties: they matter for human health, and they are precisely the regions where a linear reference performs worst. They are the clearest demonstrations of where a pangenome is most valuable, and they are the natural home for a problem set built around real questions in human health.

## 13. Ethics, consent, and data sovereignty

A pangenome is a claim about whose genomes constitute a species' reference picture, which makes it an ethical object as much as a technical one. The history of human genomics is also a history of whose diversity was represented and whose was not, of consent given and consent absent, and of the sovereignty communities hold over their own genomic data. A graduate treatment should give this its own grounding rather than a closing disclaimer.

There is also a point here that is scientific and not only ethical, and the two reinforce each other. A pangenome represents only the haplotypes chosen to build it, so its usefulness for any given individual or population is bounded by the composition of the panel. Reference bias does not disappear with the move to a pangenome; it is transformed into panel-composition bias. The remedy for the technical problem and the remedy for the ethical problem turn out to be the same: build pangenomes from broad, equitably sourced, consented diversity. Representation is at once a question of justice and a question of accuracy.

## 14. Synthesis and outlook

The arc of this review runs from one genome to many, and from a reference to a resource. It began with reference-guided resequencing and the distortions of a single linear reference, defined the pangenome as a multi-assembly, alignment-based representation of a species' variation, rooted in the core and accessory distinction and realized, but not exhausted, by graphs. Genomic anatomy was shown to govern how graphs can be built; the two dominant construction philosophies were shown to trade usability against faithful representation; and the resulting structures were shown to be visualizable, convertible into transcriptomes and gene graphs, usable as alignment targets, personalizable into individual genomes, genotypable, and capable of carrying functional measurement into sequence the reference cannot reach.

Two purposes run beneath all of it. A pangenome is a representation of evolutionary history, a way of studying variation itself, and at the same time a resource for performing genomics on individuals. The construction paradigms, the analysis choices, and even the ethical questions arrange themselves along that distinction. Non-human pangenomes are not a separate topic but a recurring instrument: different species' pangenomes illuminate different facets, and the mouse pangenome in particular is the clearest setting in which to teach personalization and inference.

The open problems follow directly from the limitations named along the way: graph-native functional analysis to replace the current reliance on surjection; implicit-graph strategies to allow the more faithful, self-similarity-aware representations to scale; and pangenomes built from genuinely representative, consented human diversity, so that the move beyond a single reference delivers on its promise rather than re-encoding old biases in new structures. The single reference was the starting point the field required. The pangenome is what it becomes once that starting point is no longer mistaken for the whole.
